# Florida SMB Workflow Audit — Presenter Script

**Use:** Read this aloud on discovery calls, in-person meetings, or while clicking through the HTML deck.  
**Time:** ~12–15 minutes full · ~5 minutes short · ~90 seconds Apollo opener  
**Deck:** [FLORIDA_SMB_WORKFLOW_AUDIT_PITCH_DECK.html](./FLORIDA_SMB_WORKFLOW_AUDIT_PITCH_DECK.html)

**Rule:** One breath between slides. Every transition ends with a question or pause — never rush from stat to stat.

---

## The arc (memorize this)

```
Hook → Their pain → Why it costs (research) → Not a software pitch
  → Five beats (shared language) → Florida / their size → Their vertical
  → The audit → What they get → Optional path → Why Fikiri → 90-day picture → Close
```

## Full script (slide by slide)

### Slide 1 — Open

**Transition in:** *(No intro needed — start with the question on screen.)*

> Thanks for making time. I’ll keep this tight — about fifteen minutes, and I want to leave room for your questions.
>
> Here’s where I’d like to start. **When a customer inquiry comes in — email, form, text, voicemail — where does it go after it hits your inbox?**
>
> Not what your website says. What actually happens on a busy Tuesday.
>
> That’s what we map. We’re Fikiri Solutions — Florida-based — and we help service businesses fix **one workflow** at a time: inquiries, quotes, follow-ups, scheduling — **before** anyone buys more software.

**Pause.** Let them answer briefly or nod. Don’t solve yet.

**Bridge to Slide 2:**  
> What you describe — or what we see when owners are honest — usually sounds like this.

---

### Slide 2 — Sound familiar

> Quotes and requests land in five different places, and **nobody owns the queue**.
>
> Follow-ups happen when someone remembers — not on a cadence.
>
> The CRM or the spreadsheet is always a week behind the inbox.
>
> Scheduling is phone tag.
>
> And when anything breaks, **the owner is the backup**.
>
> **Which of those is loudest for you right now?** Pick one — that’s the workflow we’d map if we work together.

**Listen.** Write down their words. Use their vocabulary for the rest of the call.

**Bridge to Slide 3:**  
> That pattern feels like “just how business runs.” But it’s not free — and the cost shows up in research, not just frustration.

---

### Slide 3 — The real cost

> Manual workflows don’t feel expensive day to day. They feel like **being busy**.
>
> But three things stack up.
>
> **First — speed.** Harvard Business Review audited over **two thousand** U.S. companies on how fast they respond to web inquiries. Average response time? **Forty-two hours.** Almost a quarter **never responded at all.** Companies that replied within **one hour** were **seven times** more likely to have a real conversation with the decision-maker — not just leave a voicemail.
>
> **Second — follow-up.** A separate study of **three and a half million** leads found that **half** of prospects never get a **second** contact attempt. Yet **ninety-three percent** of people who eventually buy were reached by the **sixth** attempt. Most shops stop at one.
>
> **Third — your time.** NFIB — the small-business federation — estimates customer and client paperwork runs about **forty-three dollars an hour** of owner time, and in nearly **half** of small firms, **the owner** still handles that work.
>
> So if your team — mostly you — spends even **eight hours a week** on routing inquiries, follow-ups, and CRM updates, that’s roughly **eighteen thousand dollars a year** in time alone. And if better follow-up wins you **one extra job a month** at twenty-five hundred dollars, that’s **thirty thousand** in revenue on the **same** marketing spend — not a promise, a scenario we’d validate with your numbers.
>
> **Does that order of magnitude feel high, low, or about right for your shop?**

**Bridge to Slide 4:**  
> I’m not here to throw stats at you and sell a platform. Let me be clear about what this is.

---

### Slide 4 — Not a software pitch

> We’re **system fixers first.** Software is the *how* — **after** we find the leak.
>
> We don’t automate everything. We don’t force you into a CRM shape that doesn’t match how you actually work.
>
> We map **one workflow** end to end — in plain language.
>
> You get a **do this next / not yet** list — including what **not** to touch yet.
>
> And anything customer-facing stays **human-approved.** No robot emails behind your back.
>
> If you’ve been burned by a demo that looked great and never fit your operation — that’s exactly why we start with diagnosis, not licenses.

**Bridge to Slide 5:**  
> When we diagnose, we use the same five beats every service business runs — whether you’re landscaping, real estate, or a clinic.

---

### Slide 5 — Five beats

> Think of your business as five beats:
>
> **Input** — where work comes in: calls, forms, email, referrals.
>
> **Decision** — quote, schedule, assign the crew, go or no-go.
>
> **Execution** — the visit, the job, the showing, the booking.
>
> **Follow-up** — confirmations, reminders, status, reviews.
>
> **Money** — invoice, deposit, did we actually get paid, do we know our margin.
>
> When something hurts, it’s usually a **break between two beats** — inquiry came in, but never became a decision; quote went out, but follow-up never happened.
>
> **For you, which handoff breaks most often?** Input to decision, or decision to follow-up?

**Bridge to Slide 6:**  
> We do this work with Florida service businesses your size — and you’re in a segment the Labor Department tracks closely.

---

### Slide 6 — Florida / your size

> Small businesses employ **sixty-two million** Americans — almost **forty-six percent** of the workforce. The Bureau of Labor Statistics tracks firms in the **1–49** and **50–249** employee bands separately because behavior changes as you grow.
>
> Our sweet spot is **eleven to fifty** employees: big enough that workflow pain is real, small enough that the owner still routes every inquiry — **before** a full ops hire pays for itself.
>
> Construction, facilities, real estate, automotive, clinics, hospitality, consumer services — different words, same five beats.
>
> **Where would you put yourself on that list?**

**Bridge to Slide 7:**  
> Let me translate the framework into your world.

---

**Real estate example:**

> For you, it’s an **inquiry or showing request.** First response is **qualify and get a calendar hold** — not “we’ll get back to you.”

**Construction / trades:**

> Estimate or emergency call in → **dispatch or book the consult.** The leak is usually who sees it first.

**Bridge to Slide 8:**  
> Here’s exactly what we do in the first conversation — thirty minutes, **one workflow**, no scope creep.

### Slide 8 — The audit

> We ask six things:
>
> Where do opportunities **actually** show up — and how many per week?
>
> **Who** owns first touch — and what happens when that person is out?
>
> How long to a **meaningful** first response — not an auto-reply?
>
> Where’s the **official record** — or is it “in my inbox”?
>
> Is follow-up **templated or improvised**?
>
> And where did it **break last month** — missed call, bad handoff, quote gone cold?
>
> **Parking lot rule:** other ideas go on a list. We don’t try to fix everything in one meeting.
>
> **Think of last month — one job or quote that slipped. What happened?**

**Bridge to Slide 9:**  
> You don’t walk away with vague advice. You get documents you can use.

---

### Slide 9 — Deliverables

> After a Workflow Diagnostic you get:
>
> A **process map** — current state and recommended future state.
>
> An **automation scorecard** — volume, structure, risk.
>
> A **savings estimate** — hours and dollars, assumptions on **one page**, using **your** volume and ticket size.
>
> A clear **recommended next step** — foundations, one automation, or **not yet**.
>
> And a **“not yet” list** — what we will **not** do next, and why. That alone saves most owners from buying the wrong tool.
>
> Your time: about **two hours** total including prep. Fixed scope — not an open-ended consulting meter.

**Bridge to Slide 10:** *(Skip or skim on cold calls.)*

> If the diagnostic shows you’re ready to build, there’s a path — but only in order.

---

### Slide 10 — Optional path *(discovery calls only)*

> **Phase one** — diagnostic: find the leak.
>
> **Phase two** — foundations: make CRM and email **trustworthy** — ownership, stages, dedupe.
>
> **Phase three** — automation build: **one** workflow shipped, tested, trained.
>
> We don’t automate broken routing or mystery data. That’s how you pay twice.

**Bridge to Slide 11:**  
> When you do build, here’s how we’re different from enterprise CRM theater.

### Slide 11 — Why Fikiri

> **Human in the loop** — AI drafts and sorts; your team approves.
>
> **Gmail and Outlook** — not rip-and-replace.
>
> **Lightweight CRM** — contacts tied to real conversations.
>
> **Florida-based** — we know seasonal swings, tourism, property, service-area ops.
>
> **Fixed phases** — named deliverables every step.

**Bridge to Slide 12:**  
> So what does “fixed” look like ninety days later — and what does the research say is realistic?

### Slide 12 — Success / benchmarks

> In ninety days, one workflow fixed beats ten tools added.
>
> Inquiry hits **one queue** with a named owner and a backup rule.
>
> First response becomes **measured** — not “when someone sees the phone.”
>
> Quotes get **follow-up two and three** on a cadence.
>
> The CRM or sheet **matches the inbox** — one record per customer.
>
> You review a **weekly report**, not every text thread.
>
> The benchmarks back this up: **seven times** more likely to qualify inside an hour; **half** of businesses never make a second attempt; sellers spend **seventy percent** of their time on non-selling work — admin, CRM, email.
>
> Plug in **your** numbers: five hours a week saved at that NFIB rate is about **eleven thousand a year** in time. One extra job a month at **your** average ticket — you can do that math faster than I can.
>
> **What’s your average job size and roughly how many quotes go out a month?**

**Bridge to Slide 13:**  
> From here, three outcomes — and I’m fine with any of them.
