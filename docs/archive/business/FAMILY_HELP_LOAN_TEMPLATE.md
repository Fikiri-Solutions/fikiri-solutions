**Events of default (examples):** (a) any payment is **more than 30 calendar days** late; (b) full amount not paid by **Maturity Date**; (c) Borrower materially breaks this note (including using the loan for something other than the stated purpose if the Lender cares to treat that as default — optional).

**Cure:** Lender may send **written notice** of default. Borrower has **15 calendar days** after that notice to pay all missed amounts, late fees, and bring the loan current (unless the default is failure to pay at Maturity — then see below).

**No waiver:** Lender accepting a late payment does **not** waive future rights unless **Lender says so in writing**.

## 3. What interest can you offer? (plain language)

These are **common patterns** for private / family loans; caps and rules differ by place.

- **Simple yearly rate (APR):** You agree on **X% per year** on the principal. Example: **$5,000** at **6% simple** for one year ≈ **$300** interest; total payback **$5,300** if paid at the end of the year (your schedule can split it).

- **Flat “thank-you” dollar amount:** Instead of %, you add a fixed **$Y** on top of principal by the maturity date. Easy to explain; total is fixed.

- **Match a reference rate:** Some people prefer the rate to be close to **IRS Applicable Federal Rates (AFR)** for the U.S. (short / mid / long term) so the loan does not look like a disguised gift — **ask a tax professional** if the loan is large or long.

- **0% interest:** Possible for smaller or short loans, but for **large** amounts or long terms, get **tax advice** (gift / imputed interest rules).

Pick a rate and term you can **actually** hit; put the **maturity date** and **total** in the note so the Lender sees the full picture.
